    Our Mission

    How can we decrease organic food waste and increase environmentally friendly farming practices in the Kibera Community?

    EarthPulse

    EarthPulse is a youth-led environmental initiative based in Mombasa, Kenya, that addresses one of the country’s overlooked challenges: the mixing of organic food waste with regular trash. In many households, leftover food is discarded in landfills, where it releases methane, a potent greenhouse gas that contributes to climate change.

    Through its Youth Empowerment program, EarthPulse educates communities on how to separate organic waste, compost it locally, and produce natural fertilizer that supports sustainable farming. By combining environmental education, innovation, and youth leadership, the initiative is transforming how people view waste and fostering a culture of sustainability that begins at the community level.

    Our Goals

    Spread Awareness on Organic Waste Segregation

    We noticed a lack of segregation in many households, with organic food waste mixed into regular trash. To change this, we conducted awareness drives through door-to-door visits, visual demonstrations, and community sessions.

    In the video on the right, you can see how during our first drive, the EarthPulse youth had to personally segregate the waste themselves because there was little to no awareness at the time.

    We shared how separating organic waste protects the environment and reduces harmful effects like methane emissions. Using Swahili slogans such as “Tenganisha taka, linda mazingira,” we connected with families on a personal level.

    Many households began keeping separate bins for food waste, showing a real shift toward cleaner and more sustainable living.

    Youth Empowerment

    We united passionate youth from across Mombasa - Changamwe, Likoni, Nyali, and Kisauni - under one banner: EarthPulse Youth. A movement built on action, not talk.

    Our first drive brought them together for organic waste segregation and awareness, where they led by example, distributing eco-bags, educating families, and sorting waste that had never been separated before. In the photos and videos, you can see their energy, youth from different backgrounds working as one team for the planet.

    GreenForce became more than a group, it's a force of youth empowerment, leadership, and environmental pride. Through collaboration and real action, they proved that young people can drive lasting community change.

    The movement continues to expand, with mentorship and engagement focused on empowering youth in Kibera to lead their own waves of environmental action, by the youth, for the community.

    Turning Waste into Worth

    Using the organic food waste collected, we successfully operated a community composting machine that transforms everyday kitchen waste into rich, chemical-free compost.

    Our process was entirely hands-on and community-driven:
    1. Collect: Only organic waste from homes, canteens, and markets.
    2. Sort & Shred: Break down waste for faster composting.
    3. Layer: Combine greens (wet waste) with browns (dry materials).
    4. Turn Regularly: Add oxygen to speed up decomposition.
    Wait 4–6 Weeks: The result — dark, nutrient-rich compost ready for use.

    Building on this success, we’re now planning to scale the model once additional funds are secured by installing community composting machines across Mombasa, starting with one composting unit per residential building. Each unit would serve around 50–60 households, allowing families to manage their own organic waste sustainably while contributing to a cleaner, greener city.

    Educate Youth and Families on Sustainability

    We organized interactive workshops across several communities, engaging students, parents, and vendors. Participants brought their own kitchen waste and learned how to segregate, compost, and reuse effectively.

    Through these sessions, families understood that sustainability starts at home. The workshops also introduced urban gardening using the compost we produced — encouraging communities to grow fresh, chemical-free food while reducing waste.
